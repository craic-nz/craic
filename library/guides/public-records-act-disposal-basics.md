# Public Records Act 2005: Disposal Basics

> A plain-language quick reference to what a public record is, why you may not destroy one without authority, and how disposal, disposal authorities, and transfer work under the Public Records Act 2005.

## Overview

The Public Records Act 2005 governs how public sector organisations create, manage, keep, and dispose of their records. Its purposes include enabling the Government to be held to account through full and accurate records, and providing for the preservation of, and public access to, records of long-term value.

The Act is administered by the Department of Internal Affairs. Day-to-day regulatory leadership sits with the Chief Archivist and Archives New Zealand, who set standards, monitor compliance, issue disposal authorities, and provide guidance to the sector.

The single most important rule for practitioners is simple: records of continuing value must be kept, and no public record may be destroyed or otherwise disposed of without the authority of the Chief Archivist. Disposal is a controlled, authorised process, not a matter of individual discretion.

## Key points

### What is a public record

A public record is information, in any form, created or received by a public office in the course of its business. The definition is deliberately broad and is about content and function, not format.

- It covers documents, emails, datasets, databases, images, audio, social media content, and information held in line-of-business systems, not just paper files.
- It applies regardless of where the information is stored, including cloud services and third-party systems.
- Public offices have a statutory obligation to create and maintain full and accurate records of their affairs, in line with normal prudent business practice. Records management is a legal duty, not optional housekeeping.

### Disposal is more than destruction

"Disposal" in the Act is a broader idea than simply deleting or shredding. Disposal decisions determine the ultimate fate of a record once it is no longer needed for current business.

| Disposal outcome | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| Retain then destroy | Keep the record for a set minimum period, then destroy it once authorised |
| Retain as archive | Keep the record permanently because it has long-term value |
| Transfer | Move control of the record to another body, including transfer to the Chief Archivist |

"Sentencing" is the act of applying a disposal authority to records to decide which outcome and retention period applies to each class.

### The prohibition on disposal without authority

The Act prohibits disposal without authority. No person may dispose of, or authorise the disposal of, public records except with the authority of the Chief Archivist.

- This protects the public record against premature, accidental, or self-interested destruction.
- It applies to deletion in digital systems just as it applies to physical destruction. Pressing "delete" on a record without a valid authority is non-compliant.
- Unauthorised disposal is a serious matter and can expose an organisation and individuals to scrutiny and sanction.

### Disposal authorities

A disposal authority is the instrument that gives lawful permission to dispose of records. It specifies classes of records, the disposal action, and the retention period.

- **General Disposal Authorities (GDAs)** are issued by the Chief Archivist and apply across the public sector to common, routine classes of records (for example, certain administrative and corporate records). They give standing authority that all relevant public sector organisations may rely on.
- **Agency-specific disposal authorities** cover records that are unique to a particular organisation's functions. These are developed by the organisation and approved by the Chief Archivist.
- Records must not be disposed of until a current, applicable authority is in place. Where no authority covers a class of records, those records must be kept until one is obtained.

### The role of the Chief Archivist and Archives New Zealand

- The Chief Archivist is the statutory authority for disposal and the source of all disposal authorities.
- Archives New Zealand sets recordkeeping standards, monitors and reports on compliance, issues guidance, and stewards records of long-term value transferred into its care.
- Archives New Zealand also operates as the national archive, providing for public access to records once they are open.

### Transfer of records

- Public offices must transfer records that are 25 years old to the control of the Chief Archivist, unless the records have been authorised for destruction or a deferral of transfer has been agreed.
- At or before transfer, the access status of records must be classified as either open access or restricted access, so that public access can be managed appropriately.
- Transfer moves stewardship to the national archive; it does not, by itself, make every record publicly available.

## Common pitfalls

- **Assuming "delete" is not disposal.** Deleting digital records, clearing mailboxes, or decommissioning a system all dispose of public records and all require authority.
- **Treating retention periods as deadlines to delete.** A retention period is a minimum, not a trigger to destroy on the day it expires. Disposal still requires a valid authority and a deliberate, sentenced decision.
- **Relying on an authority that does not cover the records.** A General Disposal Authority covers only the classes it names. Records outside those classes must be kept until a specific authority exists.
- **Forgetting records held by third parties.** Records held in cloud platforms or by contracted providers remain public records. Contracts should preserve the organisation's ability to manage and dispose of them lawfully.
- **Losing records by neglect rather than decision.** Failing to capture records, or letting systems be decommissioned without migration, can amount to uncontrolled loss of the public record.
- **Confusing transfer with publication.** Transfer to the Chief Archivist changes who controls the record; access status (open or restricted) is a separate decision that governs who can see it.
