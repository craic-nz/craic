# Using AI Responsibly in Information Management

> Practical, balanced guidance for records, privacy, and information security practitioners on using AI tools, including large language models, in New Zealand government work.

## Overview

Artificial intelligence, and in particular generative AI built on large language models, can help with summarising documents, drafting, classifying records, and answering questions over large bodies of text. These are exactly the tasks that information management, privacy, and information security teams handle every day, so the potential value is real.

The risks are equally real. AI tools can expose personal or classified information, can produce confident but incorrect output, can embed bias, and can move data outside New Zealand's jurisdiction in ways that conflict with legal and policy obligations. The New Zealand Public Service has issued guidance that supports agencies to adopt these tools in ways that are safe, transparent, and responsible, rather than banning them or adopting them uncritically.

This guide is a practical orientation, not a substitute for your organisation's own policy or for the central guidance referenced below. The guiding principle is straightforward: use AI to assist competent human judgement, never to replace it, and never feed it information it should not see.

## Key points

### Know what you are putting in

The most common and most serious mistake is entering information into an AI tool that should not leave the organisation's control.

- You must not put personal information into a public or consumer AI tool unless you have a clear lawful basis and your organisation's approval. The Privacy Act 2020 and its information privacy principles continue to apply to anything you do with personal information, including using it to prompt an AI system.
- You must not put classified information (IN-CONFIDENCE, SENSITIVE, RESTRICTED, or higher) into a public AI tool. Treat a prompt to an external service as a disclosure to a third party.
- Be alert to aggregation. Individually harmless facts can combine, in a single prompt or across a session, into sensitive or identifying information.

### Data sovereignty and where information goes

- Many public AI services store prompts, process them offshore, and may use them to train future models. You should assume that anything entered into a public tool may be retained and seen by the provider unless a contract says otherwise.
- Data sovereignty matters: New Zealand government information, and especially personal information about New Zealanders, may carry expectations and obligations about where it is held and who can access it. Prefer tools and contractual arrangements that keep government data within agreed jurisdictions and under enforceable terms.
- Use approved, enterprise-grade services with appropriate contractual and security controls (including, where required, certification and accreditation under the NZISM) rather than personal or free accounts for any work-related task involving non-public information.

### Keep a human in the loop

- AI output should be treated as a draft or a suggestion, never as a final answer. A named person remains accountable for any decision, document, or advice, regardless of how it was produced.
- Human review must be proportionate to the stakes. The higher the impact on a person or on the organisation, the more careful and qualified the review should be.
- Do not use AI to make or substantially determine decisions about individuals without appropriate oversight, transparency, and the ability to explain and challenge the outcome.

### Accuracy, hallucination, and verification

- Large language models can generate plausible but false statements, including invented citations, case references, statistics, and quotes. You must verify factual claims against authoritative sources before relying on them.
- Never present AI-generated legal, privacy, or security conclusions as authoritative without checking them against the actual legislation, standard, or policy.

### Transparency

- Be transparent, within your organisation's policy, about where and how AI has been used, particularly when it affects people or contributes to official advice or records.
- Where AI output becomes part of an official record, normal recordkeeping obligations under the Public Records Act 2005 apply. Capture and manage it as you would any other record.

### Bias and fairness

- AI systems reflect the data they were trained on and can reproduce or amplify bias. Outputs may disadvantage particular groups in ways that are not obvious.
- Consider impacts on specific communities and, in the New Zealand context, give particular attention to Maori data and to ensuring AI use does not entrench inequity. Review outputs critically for skew before acting on them.

### Security of the tool itself

- Treat AI services as part of your supply chain. They should be assessed, approved, and monitored like any other system that touches government information.
- Be alert to prompt injection and to AI features being switched on by default in everyday software. Confirm that any AI capability in your existing tools is configured in line with your organisation's policy before using it on real work.

## Common pitfalls

- **Pasting real content to "just try it".** Experimenting with live personal or classified material in a public tool is a disclosure, even if it never leaves your screen afterwards. Use synthetic or already-public data to test.
- **Trusting fluent output.** Confident, well-written text is not the same as correct text. The smoother the prose, the easier it is to skip verification.
- **Shadow AI.** Staff quietly using unapproved tools is one of the largest risks. It is better to provide an approved option and clear rules than to drive use underground.
- **Forgetting the record.** AI-assisted drafts, decisions, and analysis can be public records. Failing to capture them creates recordkeeping gaps.
- **Assuming a setting protects you.** "Do not train on my data" toggles vary by product and can change. Rely on contracts and approved services, not on consumer settings.
- **Over-correcting into a blanket ban.** Refusing all AI use does not remove the risk; it removes the visibility and the controls. The central guidance favours safe, transparent adoption over prohibition.
