# New Zealand Government Information Classification: Quick Guide

> A practitioner's quick reference to how the New Zealand Government classifies, marks, and handles official information under the Protective Security Requirements (PSR) and the New Zealand Information Security Manual (NZISM).

## Overview

The New Zealand Government Information Security Classification System is the primary way the public sector signals how much protection a piece of information needs. Classification is a judgement about the harm that would result if the information were compromised, that is, if it were lost, accessed without authority, altered, or disclosed inappropriately.

The system sits within the Protective Security Requirements (PSR), New Zealand Government's expectations for managing personnel, physical, and information security. The detailed technical controls that follow from a classification (for example how a system must be configured, encrypted, or accredited) are set out in the New Zealand Information Security Manual (NZISM).

A classification is not the same as a security clearance. The classification describes the information; a clearance describes a person. Access to higher national security classifications requires both an appropriate clearance and a genuine need to know.

Classifications must be applied on the basis of content, not convenience. Over-classification is a real cost: it restricts legitimate sharing, increases handling overhead, and undermines trust in the system. Under-classification risks harm. Practitioners should classify accurately and review classifications over time, because the sensitivity of information can fall (and the system provides for declassification).

## Key points

### The two families of classification

New Zealand uses one base level and two families of classified markings.

- **UNCLASSIFIED** is the base level. It is information that does not require a classification but may still need careful handling (for example, personal information that remains subject to the Privacy Act 2020).
- **Policy and privacy classifications** protect the public interest or personal privacy. The harm here is to good government, commercial interests, or individuals, rather than to national security.
- **National security classifications** protect the security, defence, or international relations of New Zealand. The potential harm is to the nation.

| Marking | Family | Applied when compromise would |
| --- | --- | --- |
| UNCLASSIFIED | Base level | Cause no identifiable harm requiring a classification |
| IN-CONFIDENCE | Policy and privacy | Be likely to prejudice the maintenance of law, personal privacy, commercial or legal matters, or the orderly conduct of government |
| SENSITIVE | Policy and privacy | Be likely to damage the interests of New Zealand or endanger the safety of individuals |
| RESTRICTED | National security | Be likely to adversely affect national interests in a limited way |
| CONFIDENTIAL | National security | Be likely to damage national interests significantly |
| SECRET | National security | Be likely to damage national interests seriously |
| TOP SECRET | National security | Be likely to damage national interests exceptionally gravely |

Note that RESTRICTED is a national security classification, not a policy-and-privacy one, even though in volume terms agencies handle far more IN-CONFIDENCE and SENSITIVE material. The two families are assessed against different kinds of harm, so practitioners must not treat the markings as a single ascending scale of "more or less secret".

### Endorsements

A classification can be combined with one or more endorsement markings. An endorsement does not change the classification level; it adds special handling or need-to-know dissemination requirements. Endorsements should be used only where they add a genuine handling instruction.

- **Policy and privacy endorsements** include markings such as CABINET, COMMERCIAL, POLICY, and STAFF, which flag the nature of the information and who should see it.
- **National security endorsements** include NEW ZEALAND EYES ONLY (NZEO), which restricts access to New Zealand citizens with an appropriate clearance on a need-to-know basis; RELEASABLE TO (REL TO), which names the countries or citizens to whom the information may be passed; and sensitive compartmented markings, which place information into a specific need-to-know compartment.

### Who sets classifications

- The originator of the information sets the initial classification, based on its content and the harm test. The person who creates or first receives the information is responsible for marking it correctly.
- Agency heads are accountable for having a classification policy in place and for ensuring staff are trained to apply it. Every worker has a personal responsibility to handle classified information appropriately.
- The framework itself is owned and promoted at the centre of government as part of the PSR. Agencies must implement it; they do not each invent their own scheme.

### Handling basics

- **Mark clearly.** Apply the protective marking so it is visible on the document, email, or system, typically at the top and bottom of pages and in email subject lines or banners.
- **Store and transmit to the level required.** Higher classifications attract stronger storage, encryption, and transmission controls. The NZISM sets out the technical controls that apply at each level.
- **Share on a need-to-know basis.** Classification enables safe sharing; it does not prohibit it. Share with those who need the information to do their job, and respect any endorsement.
- **Aggregate carefully.** A collection of individually low-sensitivity items can warrant a higher classification than any single item.
- **Review and declassify.** Information should not stay classified indefinitely. Reassess classification when circumstances change.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating the markings as one linear scale.** IN-CONFIDENCE and SENSITIVE address policy and privacy harm; RESTRICTED and above address national security harm. They are different tests, not simply different heights on one ladder.
- **Over-classifying for caution.** Marking everything SENSITIVE "to be safe" devalues the marking, slows legitimate work, and is itself a failure to classify accurately.
- **Confusing classification with a clearance.** Holding a SECRET-level clearance does not give a person the right to see all SECRET information; the need-to-know test still applies.
- **Forgetting the legal overlay.** A classification does not override obligations under the Official Information Act 1982, the Privacy Act 2020, or the Public Records Act 2005. An UNCLASSIFIED record can still contain personal information that must be protected, and a classified record may still be subject to a lawful request.
- **Marking the medium, not the content.** The classification belongs to the information. Copying it into a new document, email, or system carries the classification with it.
- **Leaving endorsements unmanaged.** Applying NZEO or a compartment marking without a real handling reason creates friction without adding protection.
