# Information Sharing Agreement (ISA) Drafting

> Helps you draft a non-statutory Information Sharing Agreement between New Zealand government agencies, grounded in the Privacy Act 2020 and good-practice ISA structure.

- **Use it for:** preparing a first draft of an ISA, checking that an existing draft covers the right ground, or working through whether you have lawful authority to share personal information with another agency.
- **Works well with:** any capable general-purpose AI assistant (a large language model). Pairs well with a structured ISA template from your agency.
- **Source frameworks:** Privacy Act 2020, in particular Information Privacy Principle 11 (limits on disclosure), supported by IPP 2, IPP 3, and IPP 10. The Government Chief Privacy Officer (GCPO) publishes information sharing guidance for the public sector. See the GCPO information sharing resource hub at https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/privacy-security-and-risk/privacy/information-sharing/welcome-to-the-resource-hub-for-information-sharing and the Office of the Privacy Commissioner at https://www.privacy.org.nz/

## The prompt
```
You are assisting two or more New Zealand government agencies to draft a non-statutory Information Sharing Agreement (ISA) for sharing personal information, consistent with the Privacy Act 2020.

Context you must use:
- Sharing agencies: [NAME THE ROLES, FOR EXAMPLE THE DISCLOSING AGENCY AND THE RECEIVING AGENCY, USING GENERIC ROLE LABELS]
- Purpose of sharing: [WHY THE INFORMATION IS BEING SHARED AND THE OUTCOME IT SUPPORTS]
- Information to be shared: [THE CATEGORIES OF PERSONAL INFORMATION, AND ANY SPECIAL CATEGORIES]
- People affected: [WHO THE INFORMATION IS ABOUT]
- Legal basis being relied on: [STATE THE BASIS, FOR EXAMPLE A PERMITTED GROUND UNDER IPP 11, OR SAY TO BE CONFIRMED]
- Frequency and method: [ONE-OFF OR ONGOING, AND HOW INFORMATION WILL MOVE BETWEEN AGENCIES]

Task:
1. State, as a preliminary view, whether there appears to be a lawful basis to share the information, focusing on IPP 11 disclosure and noting any reliance on IPP 2, IPP 3, and IPP 10. Flag clearly if the basis is unclear and must be confirmed.
2. Draft an ISA with these sections: parties and roles; purpose and outcomes; the information shared and what is excluded; lawful authority; how transparency to affected people is met; security and storage controls; access, correction, and complaints handling; retention and disposal; breach notification; review, variation, and termination; and signatories.
3. For each section, write clear operative wording using must, should, and may precisely.
4. Identify the privacy risks the agreement should manage, and ensure each is addressed in the draft.
5. List the open questions that the agencies and their privacy officers must resolve before signing.

Output format:
- A short legal-basis assessment, clearly marked as preliminary.
- The drafted ISA, section by section.
- A risk and open-questions list.

Rules:
- Use UK English. Use the words must, should, and may precisely.
- Use generic role labels for the agencies. Do not assume facts you were not given.
- Do not assert that the sharing is lawful. Frame the legal basis as a preliminary view for a qualified person to confirm.
```

## How to use it
1. Set out the purpose, the information, and the people affected before anything else, because these define the lawful basis.
2. If you are unsure of the basis under IPP 11, run the prompt anyway and treat the legal-basis section as a list of questions for your privacy officer.
3. Adapt the drafted sections to your agency's ISA template and house style.
4. Use the open-questions list to drive consultation between the agencies.
5. Have both agencies' privacy officers, and legal advisers where needed, review and approve the final agreement before signing.

## Notes and attribution
The Privacy Act 2020 and the Information Privacy Principles are New Zealand legislation. The Government Chief Privacy Officer (GCPO), a cross-government role hosted by the Department of Internal Affairs, publishes information sharing guidance and templates for the public sector through the resource hub at https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/privacy-security-and-risk/privacy/information-sharing/welcome-to-the-resource-hub-for-information-sharing . The Office of the Privacy Commissioner, an independent body, publishes guidance on the Privacy Act 2020 and the Information Privacy Principles at https://www.privacy.org.nz/ . An ISA is a non-statutory agreement and is distinct from an Approved Information Sharing Agreement made under Part 7 of the Privacy Act 2020, which follows a separate statutory process. Personal information and draft terms may be sensitive, so check your tool's data-handling settings before pasting them in. AI output must be reviewed by a qualified privacy practitioner. This resource is general information only and is not legal advice.
